NOTE: each element has a myraid of attributes that correspond with them. "Gender" is the only attribute to appear across multiple elements. This is due to the significant role that gender and sexuality played in our research.

Structural Elements
Story: the root element of all of our XMLs
Title: contains the title text for the various fairytales
P: contains paragraphs, or any plain text separated by two new lines
Quote: contains any text nested within quotations (most commonly dialogue)
The "Characteristic" Element Corresponding Attributes
Characteristic: contains sentences or phrases that define a character's persona: what are they like? How are they defined? Char_type: defines whether the character was being defined based on "physical" or "nonphysical" attributes. This helped demonstrate where emphasis was placed on physical attraction.
Depiction: determines how a character is being portrayed. We had a multitude of attributes corresponding with this element, including:
  • "beauty" -- praising outward appearance
  • "naivity" -- being unaware or unsure
  • "innocent" -- shown as sweet, pure, soft, harmless
  • "maternal" -- a character acting noticeably mother-like
  • "emotional" -- crying, anger, overflow of emotion
  • "clever" -- when a character uses wit, smarts
  • "secluded" -- alone, shut out from society
  • "protective" -- looking out for someone, unselfish
  • "brave" -- fearlessness, strength
  • "domesticity" -- being bound to the home and doing housework (a role commonly tied to women)
Gender: defines the gender ("male"/"female", since those were the only genders shown in our stories) of who is being characterized
Age: defines whether the person being characterized was "young" or "old"
The "Autonomy" Element Corresponding Attributes
Autonomy: contains sentences or phrases that demonstrate a character's freedom levels Auto_type: what type of autonomy did the character have? Were they "controlled" or "free"?
Level: who was restricting/enabling their autonomy? There were a number of outside forces that determined autonomy and were reflected in our attributes, which were:
  • "male_savior" -- was a male character saving someone/demonstrating patriarchal power?
  • "damsel" -- was a female character being depicted as hepless/someone who needs saving?
  • "evil" -- was a character purposefully being personified as evil and restricting others?
  • "religious" -- was a character being restrained based on religious powers and beliefs?
  • "societal_expectations" -- did social norms (gender roles, expectations, etc.) determine how a character had to act?
  • "heroin" -- was the savior ever female?
Gender: the gender of whose autonomy is being defined, "male"/"female"
The "Villainization" Element Corresponding Attributes
Villainization: contains sentences or phrases that portray a character as an antagonist/villaian Cause: the reason that the person was being villainized, which may have included:
  • "competition" -- vieiwing another character as a rival and thus bringing them down
  • "jealousy" -- being upset that another character has something they don't (beauty, power, wealth)
  • "revenge" -- getting someone back for a previous wrongdoing
  • "unspecified" -- the character is just seemingly evil with no defined reason
  • "sex" -- driven by sexual desires
  • "manipulation" -- using trickery to overpower someone
  • "power" -- villainized because of strength
Gender: the gender of who is being villainized, "male"/"female"
The "Reproduction" Element Corresponding Attributes
Reproduction: contains sentences or phrases that demonstrate reproductive imagery or symbolism. This is particularly because childbirth and menstruation was a vital aspect in how women are perceived in previous societal climates and folklore. Rep_type: the attribute used to define which aspect of reproduction was being alluded to or spoken about:
  • "menstruation" -- use of the color red, coming of age/inevitability of becoming a woman
  • "childbirth" -- rebirth imagery, new life
  • "sex" -- indulging in sexual desire, nakedness, pleasure
The "Adjective" Element Corresponding Attributes
Adjective: contains one-two words; wrapped around a small adjective describing a character or their actions Gender: gender of who is being described, "male"/"female"

For our textual examinations, each group member chose to study one traditional folktale, including: Snow White, Sleeping Beauty, Red Riding Hood, Rapunzel, and Beauty and the Beast. We each examined the origianl forms of these stories, working with original (often French) translations, taken from the Gutenberg Files, and adaptations written by the German-born Grimm Brothers. We marked up each of the documents in individual XML files. The goal in said markup was to examine the characteristics typically attributed to women: when they were villainized, how their autonomy differed from their male counterparts, where value was placed in their existence and how these stories provided a lens into society. We used our markup to highlight any mentions of female reproduction and domesticity to elicit themes of female inferiority on the basis of natural biological processes. By examining translations written by other authors of varying languages, we noted cultural differences in female characterization. Thus, by using consistent markup across all of our XML files, we were able to examine the ways gendered langauge changed from culture-to-culture, and the implications that these changes suggested.
