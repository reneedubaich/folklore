Abstract

Our goal with this project was to inspect the representation of women across time and culture. By looking at different translations of classic tales, we were able to research the focal point of female identity within different societies. This aligns with historical expectations of female identity: docile, submissive women whose value is placed on their beauty and ability to be a mother. By decoding the specific language used, we were able to quanitfy the emphasis placed on certain female qualities, using this as a measure of what female characteristics were most important. We found that the female identity is often a contradictory one: while you are innocent, young and lovable, once you are past the ripeness of your youth, women are often villainized and secluded, either as a helpless elderly woman or an intimidating, controlling witch.

Analysis of Grimm Autonomy Graph

As illustrated through this graph, the majority of stories contain significantly more mentions of women as being "controlled" over "free". These results are perfectly in line with our hypothesis of women as being primarily dependent, subjugated characters in Grimm fairytales. The graph seems to depict two exceptions to this rule:


The first story which seemingly counters our argument, "Little Briar Rose", while seemingly counterintuitive, actually supplements it. In the former, all instances of women as being "free" are in reference to the antagonist of the story: Sleeping Beauty's stepmother, who wishes to kill both Sleeping Beauty (known as Briar Rose in the Grimm rendition) and her children. Her motivations for wanting to kill the protagonist and her children (the antagonist's grandchildren) come from her vanity, and her obsessive desire to maintain control over the kingdom. Her villainization is in line with not only our belief that women in fairytales are depicted as being without autonomy, but also that female relationships are made to be solely negative; with women being pitted against each other due to unspecified or ungrounded jealousy or envy.


The second story whose graph is seemingly an outlier is "The Singing, Springing Lark". The majority of this story follows the character Beauty on a quest to find her husband Beast and free him from his curse. Although her main goal does revolve around a man, Beauty is the hero of this story. This remains true from the beginning where we see her volunteer herself as a prisoner in exchange for her father's life, until the end where she frees Beast from his curse. This story flips the typical narrative of the man saving the woman, although still contains many negative depictions of women commonly found in fairy tales. For example, the only other time a woman has free autonomy is when they are evil. We see this with the main villain of the story, the princess. Her actions, while of free will, mainly include kidnapping and drugging Beast. This more closely aligns with the general trend in our data where women with autonomy are depicted negatively.


This graph demonstrates the lack of female autonomy -- specifically for protagonist female characters. This is seen especially in "Little Red-Cap," a story where women are threatened by a male presence (the wolf), consumed by male power, and only freed with the help of another man. Little Red-Cap, the protagonist, is portrayed as a helpless, naive young girl; until she is consumed by the wolf. Thus, with the language in the story and symbolism of menstruation, this story can be read through a lens of sexual maturation, where Red-Cap (she loses the "little" title after being eaten and consequently released from the wolf) only gains the ability to act "clever" once being exposed to certain situations. This is a damning expectation, portraying woman as ignorant to the world unless having seen it. However, even once Red-Cap gains some knowledge -- a feat which may seem empowering -- she seems to be trapped in a life of domesticity. She says that she will "never by [her]self leave the path," which could be interpreted as her never wanting to run into a male presence again. "Little Red-Cap" thus turns into a story whose morals tell the reader, specifically young girls, to stick to the role of domestic life and never act on sexual urges, even when confronted by a man. The story warns women about the "threat" of men, which is interesting in a patriarchal society, but still locks women in a life of strict social roles. And still, Red-Cap isn't the only woman who is subject to a lack of autonomy, but her grandmother is as well. With the trope of sexual maturity, and the belief that women are only worthy while they are young, plump and fertile, older women are often cast into one of two roles: the powerful, hard, revenge-seeking witch, or the secluded, dry, decrepit old-lady. In "Little Red-Cap," there is no evil step-mother, but instead, there's the secluded grandmother, who is "ill and weak," and must be brought food by the young girl. The grandmother's dependence on others is something that society seems to cast on her -- she lives in the middle of the woods even though she is "too weak and cannot get up," yet nobody puts her closer to town; she is also eaten by the wolf and saved by a passing-by male presence. The grandmother is wise, and warns Red-Cap about the wolf in the end of the story, yet is still subject to a life of isolation despite her knowledge. This demonstrates the seeming female purpose, which doesn't extend beyong reproduction and fertility.
